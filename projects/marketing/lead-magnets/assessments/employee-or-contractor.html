Is this worker an employee (W‑2) or a contractor (1099)?

Getting this wrong is one of the most costly mistakes a business can make. Answer seven simple questions and see which one the person looks like.

By JK Accounting Group · For business owners in the US

Answer these seven questions

Think about one person who works for you. Just say yes or no. Nothing is saved and no email is needed.

1. Do you set the hours they work?

2. Do you decide how the work is done, not just what needs doing?

3. Do you give them the tools or equipment for the job?

4. Do they work only for you, and not for other clients?

5. Do you pay them a regular wage (weekly or monthly), not per project?

6. Is the work ongoing, with no set end date?

7. Do you train them in how to do the job your way?

Answer all seven to see the answer.

    Why this matters

    In plain words, so you do not get caught out.

    • The IRS decides by the facts, not the label. Calling someone a "contractor" does not make them one. What matters is how much control you have over their work.
    • The more control you have, the more it looks like an employee. You set the hours, the way, the tools, and it is ongoing — that points to an employee (W‑2).
    • Getting it wrong is expensive. If you pay an employee as a contractor, the IRS can charge back taxes and penalties. This is a common and costly mistake — worth getting right from the start.
