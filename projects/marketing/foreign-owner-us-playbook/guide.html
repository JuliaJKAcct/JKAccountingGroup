What this guide is — and how to use it

Most foreign owners we meet did the hard part right. They had a good idea, they were brave enough to build it in a new country, and they worked. What tripped them up was never the business. It was the small, invisible rules — a form they never heard of, a box they didn't know to check, a tax that showed up a year late.

This guide puts those rules in one place, in plain words. It walks the whole path: decide, set up, file, stay safe. You do not need to read it all at once. Use the menu to jump to what you need. Tick off the checklist as you go.

The good news, up front

Almost every expensive mistake in this guide is completely avoidable once you know it exists. That is the whole point of reading it. None of this is meant to scare you — it is meant to take the worry off your plate.

The three numbers below come up again and again. Keep them in mind — the rest of the guide explains each one.

$25,000
The fine for missing one US form (5472) that most foreign owners never hear about. Even if your company earned nothing.
30%
The default US tax held back on certain money paid out of the country — unless your home country's treaty lowers it.

Part 02

Before you set up

Three decisions to get right before you file any paperwork. Getting these right is cheaper than fixing them later.

Chapter 1

How a foreign owner is taxed in the US

The first question everyone asks is: "Will I be taxed twice — here and at home?" The honest answer is: it depends on how your money is earned. The US sorts your income into two buckets, and they are treated very differently.

The two buckets, in plain words

Business income (the tax word is "effectively connected income," or ECI). This is money your active US business earns — sales, services, work. You are taxed only on the profit (income minus expenses), at normal tax rates, and you file a US tax return.

Passive income (the tax word is "FDAP"). This is money like dividends, interest, or royalties paid out of the US to you abroad. It is usually taxed at a flat 30% of the full amount (no expenses subtracted) — and it is held back before it reaches you. Your home country's tax treaty can lower this a lot.

Why this matters so much: with business income you get to subtract your costs and you are taxed on what's left. With passive income the US can take 30% off the top. How you set up your company decides which bucket your money falls into — which is exactly why Chapter 2 matters.

About being "taxed twice"

The US has tax treaties with more than 60 countries, made to stop the same money being taxed twice. Whether one helps you — and by how much — depends on your country and your exact income. This is one of the most valuable things to check with an advisor early, because it can change your whole setup.

Chapter 2

Which company type is right for you

In the US you will hear three names over and over: LLC, C-Corporation, and S-Corporation. Here is the part almost no one tells foreign owners plainly:

The S-Corp trap

If you live outside the US (a "nonresident"), you cannot own an S-Corporation. It is not allowed by law. Many owners are told "an S-Corp saves the most tax" and try to set one up — for a person living abroad, it simply is not an option. Don't lose time chasing it. Your real choice is usually between an LLC and a C-Corporation.

"Double taxation" — what it really means

A C-Corporation pays 21% tax on its profit. Then, if it sends that profit to you as a dividend, that payment can be taxed again (see Chapter 11). An LLC usually avoids that second layer — but it puts the US filing on you personally. Neither is "better." The right one depends on your plan, your country, and whether you'll keep money in the US or take it home.

Chapter 3

Which state to register in

You will read online that everyone should register in Delaware. For a big company raising money from investors, sometimes yes. For most foreign owners running a real business, this is a myth that costs money.

The "Delaware for everyone" trap

If you register in Delaware but actually work from Florida, you often have to register again in Florida as a "foreign" company (foreign to that state) — paying two sets of fees and filing in two states, for no benefit. Register where you actually do business.

The simple rule: register in the state where you (or your work, office, inventory, or team) will actually be. If that is Florida, register in Florida. You can always change later if your plans grow.

You may also need a local business license — a small local permit to operate, separate from the state and the IRS. It varies by city and county. We keep a free tool for this; the link is at the end.

Part 03

Setting it up

The paperwork, in the order that actually works. Each of these has a step that trips up foreigners — we mark it.

Chapter 4

Get an EIN — without a US social security number

What an EIN is

An EIN (Employer Identification Number) is your company's tax ID number with the IRS. Think of it as the company's ID card. You need it to open a bank account, file taxes, and hire.

The myth: "I can't get an EIN because I don't have a US social security number (SSN)." Not true. Foreign owners get EINs all the time. You do not need an SSN, and you do not need an ITIN first.

  1. Fill out Form SS-4. This is the EIN application. Where it asks for the responsible party's SSN/ITIN, a foreign owner writes "Foreign."
  2. Send it in the way that works for foreigners. Online EIN applications need a US ID number, so foreign owners usually apply by fax or mail, or by phone — the IRS has a phone line for international applicants. Phone is often fastest; fax usually takes about a week; mail is the slowest.
  3. Wait for the confirmation letter (the "CP 575"). Keep it safe. Banks will ask for it.
The step people get wrong

Naming the wrong "responsible party," or filing before the company is officially formed. Get the company formed first, then apply, with you (the real owner) as the responsible party. A wrong EIN application is slow and annoying to fix.

Chapter 5

Get your ITIN (if you need one)

What an ITIN is

An ITIN (Individual Taxpayer Identification Number) is a tax ID for you as a person, when you need to file or be listed on a US tax return but can't get an SSN. The EIN is for the company; the ITIN is for you.

You do not always need one. You usually need an ITIN when you personally have to file a US tax return — for example, if your LLC's profit flows to you and you must report it. You apply with Form W-7, usually together with your tax return, and you have to prove your identity with your passport.

Don't mail your real passport

Many people mail their original passport to the IRS and then can't travel for weeks. You can avoid this by using a Certifying Acceptance Agent — a person the IRS approves to verify your passport for you, so the original stays in your hands. (We can do this.)

Chapter 6

Open a US bank account as a foreigner

This is the step that frustrates people the most, because rules differ from bank to bank and change often. Here is what is generally true.

Most banks will want to see: your company formation papers, your EIN letter, and your passport. Some traditional banks still want you to walk into a branch in person to open a business account. Others, and several online business-banking platforms, can open an account for a US company owned by a foreigner remotely — but their rules shift, and not every business type is accepted.

  • Have your formation documents and EIN letter ready as clean PDFs before you start.
  • Keep your money in the business account and your personal money separate. Mixing them is a real problem at tax time and can weaken your company's legal protection.
  • Expect questions about what your business does and where the money comes from. That's normal — have a simple, honest answer ready.
Because rules change monthly

We won't print a "best bank" list here — whatever we wrote would be out of date by the time you read it. When you get to this step, ask us which options are actually open right now for your business type and country. That one question saves weeks.

Part 04

The taxes & filings nobody explains

This is where foreign owners get surprised. None of it is hard once you know it's there. All of it is expensive to ignore.

Chapter 7

The $25,000 form most foreign owners never hear about

If a foreign person owns a US company, the IRS wants a yearly information form called Form 5472. It is not a tax bill — it is a report of the money that moved between you and your company. For a single-owner LLC it travels attached to a mostly-blank cover return (the "pro-forma" Form 1120) — your accountant files both together. But if you were supposed to file it and didn't, the fine is $25,000. Per year.

Why this catches good people

Three beliefs get owners in trouble: "My company made no money, so there's nothing to file" — wrong; even the money you put in to start the company counts as a reportable transaction. "It's just a small single-owner LLC" — a foreign-owned single-owner LLC still has to file this. "I never heard of it" — that's exactly the problem. The IRS has been sending these $25,000 notices to real owners, for years they didn't know they had to file.

The reassuring part

The form itself is free and it is just information — filing it correctly and on time is all it takes to avoid the fine. And if past years were missed, there is often a way to fix it and ask the IRS for relief. The important thing is to handle it before the IRS writes to you.

Chapter 8

Income tax & the "quarterly" surprise

The US does not wait until the end of the year for your tax. If your business will owe tax, the IRS expects you to pay it in four payments across the year ("estimated" or "quarterly" taxes). Miss them and you can owe a small penalty on top — a surprise no one enjoys.

  • A C-Corporation pays a flat 21% federal tax on its profit, and files its own return.
  • With an LLC, the profit usually flows to you, and you pay tax at the normal personal rates on that profit.
  • Your state may want its own tax too. Some states (like Florida) have no personal income tax; others do.
This is where good bookkeeping pays for itself

If your books are clean all year, these payments are simple math and there are no surprises. If your books are a mess, tax time becomes a scramble and you often overpay because you can't prove your expenses. Set it up right once, from day one.

Chapter 9

Sales tax & "nexus" — the one that hits product sellers

If you sell physical products to US customers, this chapter can be the most important one for you. The US has no single national sales tax — each state runs its own. The question is: in which states must you collect it?

"Nexus" in plain words

Nexus just means "a strong-enough connection to a state that you must collect its sales tax." You get it two ways: by being physically there (an office, a person, or inventory — including goods stored in a US warehouse for you), or by selling enough into that state.

The "selling enough" line is often around $100,000 in sales to a state in a year (some states also count 200 separate sales, though many are dropping that count). Cross the line and you're supposed to register in that state and collect its tax — even from another country.

The warehouse trap for online sellers

If you use a US fulfilment service that stores your products in its warehouses, your inventory may sit in a dozen states — and that alone can create nexus in each one, before you've sold a single "$100,000." Online sellers get caught here constantly. If this is you, get it mapped early.

If you import your products, there's a second world: customs

Sales tax is what you collect from US buyers. It is completely separate from what you pay to bring goods into the country. If you import furniture, electronics, or any physical product, you also step into customs.

Importing goods, in plain words

Customs duty / tariff is a tax you pay to bring a product into the US. The rate depends on what the product is and where it's made. Importer of record is the person or company legally responsible for that shipment — often the foreign owner, which carries real duties.

These are their own subject, with their own paperwork and their own advisors (a customs broker). We don't file customs for you — but we make sure your books and taxes handle imported inventory correctly, and we'll point you to the right customs help. If you import, raise it early: duties can change your prices and your whole plan.

Chapter 10

Hiring help: employee (W-2) or contractor (1099)?

The day you pay someone to help, the US wants to know which kind of worker they are. Get it wrong and the bill (back taxes and penalties) lands on you, the owner.

The simple difference

An employee (W-2) works under your control — you set their hours and how they work — and you must run payroll and hold back taxes for them. A contractor (1099) runs their own business and just delivers a result; you don't hold back taxes, but you report what you paid them.

The mistake owners make is calling everyone a "contractor" because it's less paperwork. The US looks at the real relationship, not the label. If you control how the work is done, they're likely an employee — no matter what the agreement says. We have a free check for this (linked at the end).

Chapter 11

Taking money out — and the treaty that can save you

Say your US company made a profit and you want to send some to yourself back home. If that payment is a dividend from a C-Corporation, the US usually holds back a flat 30% before it leaves the country. This is that "FDAP" bucket from Chapter 1.

Your country's treaty can cut the 30%

If your home country has a tax treaty with the US, that 30% is often reduced — sometimes to 15%, sometimes lower. To get the lower rate you give the payer a short form (Form W-8BEN) that names your country and the treaty. Miss that form and you pay the full 30% for no reason. This is real money, and it's exactly the kind of thing worth a five-minute check.

How you take money out (a dividend, a salary, a loan repayment, a return of your investment) changes the tax. This is one of the most personal parts of the whole guide — the right answer depends on your company type, your country, and your plans. It's a great question to bring to a call.

Chapter 13

The "days in the US" trap

Here is one that surprises people who travel to run their business. If you spend a lot of days physically in the US, the IRS can start treating you as a US resident for taxes — which can mean the US taxes your worldwide income, not just your US business. You can trigger this without any visa or green card. It's purely about days.

The rough test (the "substantial presence" rule)

Count all your days in the US this year, plus ⅓ of last year's days, plus ⅙ of the year before. If the total reaches 183 — and you were here at least 31 days this year — the US may treat you as a resident. There are exceptions (certain visas, a "closer connection" to your home country, treaties) — which is exactly why you check before it happens, not after.

The fix is almost always planning your travel and knowing your number before the year ends. We have a free day-counter tool, linked at the end.

One more thing that arrives with residency: once the US treats you as a resident, it also wants to know about your accounts back home. If all your non-US accounts together pass $10,000 at any point in the year, a yearly report (called the FBAR) is expected. It is a form, not a tax — but the fine for skipping it is real. Our "money back home" tool at the end checks this in a minute.

Chapter 14

The 10 most expensive mistakes — and how to skip them

  1. Never filing Form 5472. The $25,000 one. The fix is simply filing it, on time, every year.
  2. Chasing an S-Corp from abroad. It's not allowed for nonresidents. Choose between LLC and C-Corp instead.
  3. Registering in Delaware "because everyone does." Then paying to register again where you really work.
  4. Mixing personal and business money in one account. It breaks your books and weakens your legal protection.
  5. Ignoring quarterly taxes and getting a penalty on top of the bill.
  6. Forgetting sales tax until a state sends a letter with years of back tax owed.
  7. Calling employees "contractors" to save paperwork — then owing the back taxes yourself.
  8. Paying the full 30% on money sent home because no one filed the treaty form (W-8BEN).
  9. Spending too many days in the US and becoming a worldwide-income taxpayer by accident.
  10. Waiting until tax season to look at any of this. By then you can only report — it's too late to plan.
Notice the pattern

Every single one is cheap to prevent and expensive to fix. That's the whole reason to set things up correctly at the start — and to have someone who knows this system in your corner before decisions are made, not after.
