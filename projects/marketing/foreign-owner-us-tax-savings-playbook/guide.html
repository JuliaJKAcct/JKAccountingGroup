Planning beats reporting

Here is the quiet truth about US taxes: most of the money is saved before the year ends, not after. By the time your accountant sees your numbers in April, the year is closed — they can only report what happened. Every real saving was a decision made earlier, while there was still time to act.

That's the gap this guide fills. It is not a list of tricks or loopholes. It is the calm, legitimate set of levers — the legal switches you can choose to pull — that established owners use to keep more of their profit — the ones a year-end-only accountant never has the chance to mention, because they only meet you when it's too late.

The one question that changes everything

Before any lever, one thing decides which ones you can even use: are you a US tax resident now? Not your visa — your tax status. Many owners cross this line after a few years of spending time in the US, without noticing.

Why it matters for saving tax

Some of the biggest levers in this guide — like the S-Corporation salary strategy, and the income level where the 20% deduction starts to shrink — work differently depending on whether the US treats you as a resident. If you became a resident and didn't adjust, you may be using the wrong playbook entirely. If you're still a nonresident, some doors are closed and others open.

You don't have to figure this out alone — it's the first thing we check. As you read, notice where a lever says "if you're a US resident." Those are the ones worth a five-minute conversation about your status.

Part 02

Is your structure still right?

The biggest savings usually aren't small deductions — they're structural. You chose your setup when the business was young. It may be quietly costing you now.

Chapter 1

You may have outgrown your setup

Almost every owner picks the simplest company when they start — usually a single-owner LLC. That's often right at the beginning. But as your profit grows, the math changes, and the setup that was perfect at $40,000 of profit can be expensive at $300,000.

The lever in one line

If you keep profit in the US to reinvest, a C-Corp's flat 21% can beat paying your higher personal rate. If you're a US resident pulling steady profit, an S-Corp election can cut the payroll tax on part of it. Neither is automatic — but "should I change my structure?" is one of the highest-value questions a growing owner can ask.

Changing your tax treatment is usually a single election form — not a new company. But timing and eligibility matter, so it's worth checking before you file.

Chapter 2

How you pay yourself is a tax choice, not a habit

Most owners just move money from the business account to their own when they need it. That works — but how you label and structure those payments can change your tax bill, especially once you're a US resident.

Salary vs distribution — the payroll-tax lever

A salary carries payroll tax (about 15% on top, for Social Security and Medicare). A distribution (profit paid to an owner) usually does not. If you're a US resident running an S-Corp, paying yourself a fair salary plus taking the rest as distributions can legally cut that payroll tax — as long as the salary is genuinely reasonable for your work.

A rough, honest example: an owner with $150,000 of profit who elects S-Corp status and pays herself a fair $70,000 salary pays payroll tax on the salary — not on the other $80,000. That's often roughly $10,000–$12,000 a year of payroll tax kept, before the extra costs (a payroll service + the S-Corp tax return — together usually $1,500–$3,000 a year). Your number depends on your profit and what a fair salary is for your work — that's exactly what a review checks.

Where owners get it wrong

Paying yourself a salary that's too low to look real invites the IRS to push back — the salary has to be defensible for your role. Getting this number right is its own careful calculation; we keep a free tool that shows a fair range, linked at the end.

If you're still a nonresident, this lever looks different — it's more about your entity choice than payroll. Which is, again, why your residency status is the first thing to confirm.

Chapter 3

Where the states tax you (and where they don't)

As your business grew, it probably reached into more states — customers, a warehouse, a remote worker. Each of those can create a tax duty in that state. Two owners with the same profit can pay very different state tax, purely based on where.

  • Some states (like Florida) have no personal income tax — a real factor in where you base yourself and your company.
  • If you operate or sell across states, your profit gets split up ("apportioned") among them — and the rules differ everywhere.
  • Growing into a new state quietly adds filings. Catching them early is cheap; a letter from the state years later is not.
The move

Map where your business actually touches — people, property, and sales — once a year. It's a small review that prevents both overpaying (filing where you don't need to) and nasty surprises (missing where you do).

Part 03

The deductions & breaks owners leave on the table

This isn't about finding tricks. It's about not forgetting what's already yours to claim — and setting up the big legitimate breaks most owners never turn on.

Chapter 4

The everyday deductions people quietly miss

These are small and boring — and together they're worth thousands a year. The reason owners miss them isn't that they're hidden — it's that no one set up a clean way to capture them.

  • An accountable plan — a simple, proper way for your company to reimburse you for the home office, phone, and travel you already pay for, tax-free.
  • Business use of your car, tracked properly.
  • Start-up costs from when you launched — often deductible even a year or two later.
  • Health insurance and other benefits, structured the right way for your entity.
The catch

You can only deduct what you can prove. Every one of these needs a clean record behind it. That's why the boring habit in Chapter 11 — good books, kept as you go — is quietly the biggest money-saver of all.

Chapter 5

Deduct big purchases the same year you make them

Normally, when you buy something expensive for the business, you deduct it slowly over many years. Two rules let you deduct much of it right now instead — which lowers this year's tax.

The two rules, in plain words

Section 179 lets you deduct up to $2,560,000 of equipment in 2026, right away. Bonus depreciation — now back to 100% and permanent — lets you write off the full cost of qualifying purchases the same year, with no dollar cap. Furniture, machines, computers, vehicles, tools: often fully deductible the year you buy.

The planning move

If you were going to buy equipment anyway, when you buy it (before or after December 31) decides which year you get the deduction. In a high-profit year, buying before year-end can meaningfully cut the bill. That's a decision only planning-ahead can catch.

Chapter 6

The 20% profit deduction (now permanent)

This is one of the largest breaks for business owners, and it was just made permanent in 2025 — so you can count on it. If you qualify, you deduct up to 20% of your qualified US business profit before your tax is calculated. On $200,000 of profit, that can be up to $40,000 simply not taxed.

Does it reach foreign owners?

Yes — it applies to your US business profit that you report on a US return (your profit from actually doing business in the US — the IRS calls it "effectively connected" income), whether you're a resident or nonresident. There are limits based on your income level and the type of business — and the deduction gets smaller once your income passes a certain level, and that level is lower for a nonresident. In short: it's real, it's big, and whether you get the full 20% is worth confirming for your case.

Many owners simply never claim it, or claim less than they could because their books don't cleanly separate qualifying income. Getting it right is often a four-figure difference.

Chapter 7

Turn profit into your own retirement — before tax

This is the lever profitable owners most often never use. You can move a large slice of your profit into your own retirement account and deduct it now — you're not spending it, you're paying your future self instead of the IRS.

$72,000
The most you can put into a Solo 401(k) or SEP plan in 2026 (under 50) — deducted from this year's income.
2 types
A SEP is the simplest to open. A Solo 401(k) takes a little more setup, but lets you put in more money at the same income.
You keep it
Unlike a deduction you spend, this money stays yours — it just grows tax-deferred.
Why it's the favourite

It's the rare lever that lowers your tax and builds your wealth at the same time. Eligibility depends on your entity and your residency, and the plan usually has to be set up in the right window — so it's worth checking well before year-end, not after.

Part 04

Keeping more when the money moves

How and when profit leaves the business is itself a lever — especially for a foreign owner sending money home.

Chapter 8

Taking profit out efficiently

Getting money from your US company to yourself — and often to your home country — can be taxed a little or a lot, depending on how you do it. The same $100,000 can arrive very differently.

The treaty most owners forget to use

When a US company pays a dividend abroad, the US usually holds back a flat 30%. But if your home country has a tax treaty, that rate is often cut — sometimes to 15% or less — if you file one short form (Form W-8BEN) first. Owners lose real money every year simply by never filing it.

Beyond dividends, there are four ways to take money out — a salary, a distribution, repaying a loan you made to the company, or returning the money you originally put in. Each one is taxed differently. There's no single right answer — it depends on your entity, your country, and whether you're reinvesting or taking it home. This is one of the most personal, and most valuable, things to plan.

Chapter 9

Timing is a lever

Two businesses with identical years can pay different tax simply because one timed things and the other didn't. You often have more control than you think over which year income and expenses land in.

  • In a high-profit year, moving planned expenses into December pulls the deduction into this year.
  • Sometimes deferring income a few weeks into January moves the tax to next year.
  • Your accounting method changes which year income lands in: "cash" counts money when it arrives, "accrual" counts it when you invoice. Which one you're on is a choice worth reviewing.
If you carry inventory (products, materials, stock)

A common surprise for product businesses: you generally can't deduct inventory when you buy it — only when you sell it (as "cost of goods sold"). So buying a warehouse full of stock before year-end does not lower this year's tax the way buying equipment does.

(If you're a contractor or service business: the job materials you buy for client projects normally just count as costs when you use them — these inventory rules are about goods you hold to resell.)

The good news: smaller businesses have simpler options for how they account for inventory, and the choice can change your tax. If a real part of your business is products you hold, how your inventory is handled is worth a proper look — it's easy to get wrong in both directions.

None of this works if you find out your profit in April. It only works if someone is watching the number during the year — which is the whole point of Chapter 12.

Chapter 10

Credits worth checking

A deduction lowers the income you're taxed on. A credit is even better — it lowers the tax itself, dollar for dollar. They're narrower, but if one fits you it's real money back.

  • The R&D credit — if you build or meaningfully improve a product, software, or process, you may qualify, even if you don't think of it as "research."
  • Certain hiring credits for taking on people from specific groups.
Don't assume they're not for you

Owners routinely assume credits are only for big tech companies. Many small product and software businesses qualify and never claim. It costs nothing to check whether one fits.

Part 05

The system that makes it real — and keeps it safe

Every lever above depends on two unglamorous things: clean records, and someone looking at them before the year closes. And on staying the right side of one line.

Chapter 11

Clean books ARE the tax strategy

This is the least exciting chapter and the most important. Every deduction, every credit, every "which year" decision needs a clean record behind it. Messy books cost you twice: you overpay (because you can't prove what you spent) and you carry risk (because you can't stand behind your return).

Books done as you go, not in a panic

When your books are current every month, tax planning is just reading a number. When they're a pile of receipts in April, you take the safe, expensive path and claim less than you could. The owners who save the most aren't the most aggressive — they're the most organised.

Chapter 12

Plan quarterly, not in April

Here's the single change that turns everything in this guide from "interesting" into "money saved": look at your numbers a few times a year, while you can still act.

A short check-in each quarter answers the questions that have a deadline: Are we on track to owe more than expected? Should we buy that equipment now? Is it time to change the structure or set up the retirement plan? Are we about to trip a state filing? In April, all of those become "too late." In September, they're just decisions.

This is the difference between a preparer and an advisor

A preparer files what already happened. An advisor sits with you during the year and changes what happens next. That's the whole reason "planning beats reporting" — and it's how the levers in this guide actually reach your bank account.

Chapter 13

Smart, not aggressive

A quick word of honesty, because you'll see louder promises online. There's a real line between tax planning (using the rules the way they're meant to be used) and tax trouble (schemes that save a little now and cost a lot later). Everything in this guide is the first kind.

Don't save $1 and risk $25,000

The compliance basics still apply while you optimize. The foreign-owner form (5472) — which applies to companies with a foreign, non-US-resident owner; if you live here as a resident and fully own your company, you usually don't have it (one-minute check in the tools below). Sales tax where you have it. And paying workers correctly — if you run on subcontractors, keep a W-9 on file for each and send 1099s every January: simple when set up, expensive when misclassified. A good plan lowers your tax and keeps you safe. If a strategy only works if no one looks closely, it isn't a strategy.

Chapter 14

The 10 ways established foreign owners overpay

  1. Keeping the start-up structure long after the profit outgrew it.
  2. Pulling money out any old way instead of choosing salary vs distribution on purpose.
  3. Never claiming the 20% profit deduction — or claiming less because the books are messy.
  4. Leaving the retirement lever off — the biggest missed saving for profitable owners.
  5. Deducting big purchases slowly when they could be written off this year.
  6. Missing everyday deductions (home office, car, start-up costs) for lack of records.
  7. Paying the full 30% on money sent home because the treaty form was never filed.
  8. Ignoring timing — letting income and expenses fall into the wrong year.
  9. Overpaying state tax by not mapping where the business really operates.
  10. Only meeting the accountant in April — when it's too late to do anything but report.
Notice the pattern

Almost every one traces back to the same root: no one was looking at the numbers during the year. Fix that, and most of these fix themselves.
