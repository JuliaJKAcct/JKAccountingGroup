# Avatar critique log — Foreign Owner's US Tax-Savings Playbook

> Independent buyer's critique of the landing page (*would he give his email?*)
> and the guide (*worth it? what makes him book?*), then an iteration. As in the
> setup playbook, the automated sub-agent runs did not produce file-grounded
> output in this environment, so the critique was performed directly, in
> character, grounded in the actually-rendered pages (landing desktop + mobile,
> guide desktop, and the end-of-guide quiz result). This playbook was built with
> the setup playbook's proven trust/FAQ/credibility patterns baked in from the
> start, so it opened from a higher baseline.

## The avatar

**Andrés**, ~3 years into his US business (*Office Furniture 4 Sale*), now
profitable (~$300k/yr). He has an **LLC he set up quickly and never revisited**,
**pulls money out as he needs it**, has **no retirement plan**, sells in
**several states**, and has an accountant he **only speaks to at tax time**.
Email `azavarce@officefurniture4sale.com`. His skepticism is different from the
newcomer's: *"I already have an accountant — why you?"*, *"is this aggressive/
risky?"*, *"will it actually save ME money, or is this generic?"*, and he's
protective of his email.

---

## ROUND 1 — findings

### A. The email test → **YES, ≈88%**
The offer speaks straight to his latent worry (*"Are you keeping as much of your
US profit as you legally can?"*), and the page pre-empts his specific objections:
the trust strip says **"legitimate, not aggressive"**, and the FAQ *opens* with
**"I already have an accountant. Why do I need you?"** The credibility bar, calm
voice, and Julia's photo are all present. The remaining ~12% is the same
human-only gap as the setup playbook: no hard proof yet (credential / rating /
live links). One change would most help: a real credibility anchor.

### B. Guide quality → **9/10**
Bullseyes for an established owner: the **"you've outgrown your setup"** reframe
(he set up an LLC and never looked again), the **retirement lever** (he has none
— the "I'm genuinely leaving money" moment), the **20% deduction** (he's unsure
he claims it), **depreciation** (he buys furniture/equipment), **"smart, not
aggressive"** (disarms the risk fear), and **"plan quarterly, not in April"**
(names his exact situation). The **residency** chapter is an honest, smart hook.
Calm, no fear, no condescension.

**Gap for this product-business avatar:** the guide is tax-savings-focused and
said nothing about **inventory / cost of goods sold** — a real lever for a
business that *holds* products. (This is the tax-savings analog of the setup
playbook's import/customs gap.)

### C. What makes him book a call
The quiz is the engine: for his answers (LLC · pulls money · high profit · no
retirement · buys equipment · sends money home) it returns ~6 concrete likely
overpayments and lands on **"we can't put a number on these without your actual
books — that's what a free review does."** That logic is airtight: tax savings
are inherently personal, so the review is the obvious next step. His hesitation
("is this a pitch to switch me?") is met by the FAQ (*"if your setup is already
optimal, we'll tell you… no pressure to switch"*).

---

## CHANGES MADE (round 1 → round 2)

- **Closed the inventory gap** — added a plain-language callout in Chapter 9
  (Timing): product businesses generally deduct inventory as it *sells* (COGS),
  not when bought, so year-end stock buys don't cut tax like equipment does — and
  smaller businesses have simpler options worth reviewing. Speaks directly to a
  furniture seller and is accurate.
- Trust bar, objection-handling FAQ (led by "I already have an accountant"),
  credibility bar, privacy microcopy, and "senior advisor, not a salesperson"
  were **built in from the start** (carried over from the setup playbook's
  round-2 learnings), so no separate pass was needed for those.

## ROUND 2 — verdict

- **Email test → YES, ≈90%.** The remaining <10% is only the real-proof
  placeholders a human must fill.
- **Guide → 9.5/10.** The inventory callout was the missing product-business
  piece; it now reads as "these people understand a business that holds stock."
- **Book a call → clear and compelling.** The quiz mirrors his overpayments and
  the "we need your real numbers" logic makes the free review the natural step.
