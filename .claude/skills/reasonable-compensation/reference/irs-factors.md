# IRS Reasonable-Compensation Factors & Authority

There is **no statutory formula** for reasonable compensation. The requirement comes from
Treas. Reg. §1.162-7 (compensation must be reasonable for services actually rendered) and
from the S-corp rules that require shareholder-employees to be paid a reasonable wage
subject to employment tax before taking distributions. The IRS and the courts evaluate
reasonableness against a set of recognized **factors**. The report must walk through each
factor and tie the client's facts to it — that factor-by-factor narrative is what makes the
number defensible.

## Why it matters (the tax mechanics)

- **Salary (W-2)** is subject to FICA/Medicare payroll taxes (Social Security up to the
  wage base + 2.9% Medicare, plus 0.9% Additional Medicare over thresholds).
- **Distributions** to an S-corp shareholder are **not** subject to those payroll taxes.
- Owners are therefore tempted to minimize salary and maximize distributions. The IRS can
  **recharacterize distributions as wages**, assessing back payroll taxes, penalties, and
  interest, when the salary is unreasonably low. This is a well-established enforcement area.

## The recognized factors

Drawn from **IRS Fact Sheet FS-2008-25** and the case law below. Address each in the report:

1. **Training and experience** — the owner's education, credentials (CPA, EA, licenses),
   and years in the field. More expertise → higher reasonable comp.
2. **Duties and responsibilities** — the scope and complexity of what the owner actually
   does (the "hats"). This is the spine of the Cost-approach build-up.
3. **Time and effort devoted to the business** — hours worked; full-time vs. part-time.
4. **Dividend / distribution history** — how much the owner has taken as distributions
   vs. salary. Large distributions with a token salary is the classic red flag.
5. **Payments to non-shareholder employees** — what the company pays other staff for
   comparable work; the owner's implied rate should be coherent with these.
6. **Timing and manner of paying bonuses** to key people.
7. **What comparable businesses pay for similar services** — market comparability
   (industry, size, geography). Supplied here by BLS occupational wage data.
8. **Compensation agreements** — any written employment or compensation agreement.
9. **The use of a formula to determine compensation** — whether comp is set by a
   consistent, documented method (which is exactly what this skill provides).

## Key case law (cite as relevant)

- **David E. Watson, P.C. v. United States**, 668 F.3d 1008 (8th Cir. 2012) — CPA paid
  himself $24,000 salary while taking ~$200,000 in distributions; courts upheld
  recharacterization to a ~$91,044 reasonable salary. The leading modern case; it
  validates the replacement-cost / expert-build-up methodology.
- **Fleischer v. Commissioner**, T.C. Memo 2016-238 — reinforced substance-over-form on
  who earned the income and the need for reasonable comp.
- **Spicer Accounting, Inc. v. United States**, 918 F.2d 90 (9th Cir. 1990) — services
  rendered by an owner cannot be disguised entirely as distributions to avoid tax.
- **Radtke v. United States**, 895 F.2d 1196 (7th Cir. 1990) — sole shareholder-employee's
  distributions recharacterized as wages; a "zero salary" is not defensible.
