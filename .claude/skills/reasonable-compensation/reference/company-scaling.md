# Company Size & Complexity Scaling + Total Compensation

Two adjustments the paid reports make explicit — and we should too.

## A. Size & complexity scaling

A market wage for "General & Operations Manager" or "Marketing Manager" reflects an
*average* employer. The owner's company may be much smaller (or larger, or more complex) than
that average, and the **management/executive** roles in particular scale hard with company
size. Making the adjustment explicit is what lets a small-company salary stand on its face.

### What scales — and what doesn't
- **Management / executive / owner hats scale strongly** with revenue, headcount, and span of
  control. A $500k-revenue firm's "CEO hat" is worth a fraction of a $50M firm's.
- **Production / clerical hats (bookkeeper, admin, technician) barely scale** — a bookkeeper's
  market rate is about the same regardless of employer size. Do **not** discount these for size.

### How to apply it
1. Note the company's **size band** — revenue, number of employees, assets.
2. For each *management/executive* role, apply a documented **size factor** to the market wage,
   e.g. relative to the survey's assumed company size. Typical small-company scaling for the
   top-management hat runs **0.5–0.8×** of the general market manager wage; state the factor and
   the reason (revenue band, people supervised, single vs. multi-entity).
3. **Span of control** — few/no direct reports pulls the management wage down; supervising a
   real team pushes it up.
4. **Complexity** — multi-state, multi-entity, regulated, or high-transaction operations push
   up; a simple single-location service firm does not.
5. Show the pre-scaling wage, the factor, and the post-scaling wage so the adjustment is
   transparent. Never apply a silent discount.

> The cost approach already captures *some* of this by choosing the right occupation and
> percentile. Scaling is the **explicit, documented** layer on top — especially important for
> the management hats, which is exactly where BLS/market averages overstate a small company.

## B. Total compensation view

"Reasonable compensation" is ultimately about the **total value** the owner receives for
services — not only the cash W-2 wage. Show the full package and compare like-to-like.

### Components
- **Cash W-2 wages** — the figure subject to Social Security/Medicare; the number the IRS
  recharacterizes distributions into. **This is the headline "reasonable salary."**
- **Employer retirement contributions** — SEP/SIMPLE/401(k) employer match/profit-sharing.
- **Health & welfare benefits** — for a **>2% S-corp shareholder**, health insurance premiums
  are **added to W-2 box 1 wages** (and deducted above the line) but are **not** subject to
  FICA. Note this treatment; it affects the wage figure without affecting payroll tax.
- **Other** — HSA, life/disability, employer-paid perks.

### How to use it
- Market wage data is usually quoted as **base pay**. When comparing the owner's package to
  market, compare **base-to-base** for the reasonable-salary figure, then present **total
  compensation** (base + benefits) as context so nothing is double-counted.
- The **reasonable-salary recommendation remains the cash wage.** Benefits are shown alongside
  to complete the picture and to document that total pay is coherent with the market — they do
  **not** reduce the required cash salary.
- Flag the >2%-shareholder health-insurance W-2 mechanic explicitly; it is a common preparation
  point and clients ask about it.

Keep the distinction clean in the report: one clearly-labeled **cash reasonable salary** (the
defensible figure), with retirement and health shown as a separate **total-compensation** panel.
