# The Three Approaches & Reconciliation

Commercial reasonable-comp reports don't rely on one method — they develop **three
approaches**, then reconcile them into a single figure. Doing the same here is the largest
single jump in defensibility: three independent methods converging on a number is far harder
to challenge than one. This file defines all three and how to reconcile them.

## 1. Cost approach (many-hats) — PRIMARY for owner-operators

Already the backbone (see `methodology.md` + `wage-data.md`): break the owner into roles, price
each at a triangulated market wage, weight by hours, sum. This is the **primary** method for
service businesses and hands-on owner-operators, because their value *is* the labor they
perform. Weight it heaviest in the reconciliation for those clients.

## 2. Market approach — CORROBORATION of the total

Where the cost approach builds the number bottom-up from roles, the market approach checks the
**total** top-down: *what does the whole job — an owner-manager running a company of this type
and size — pay in the market?*

- Gather **total-compensation** comparables (not per-role) via `WebSearch`: e.g. "president /
  owner-operator / general manager total compensation" for the industry and company-size band,
  plus small-business owner compensation surveys.
- Adjust for **company size** (see `company-scaling.md`) — a $500k-revenue firm's top job pays
  a fraction of a $50M firm's.
- Produce a market-approach figure and range. It should land in the same neighborhood as the
  cost approach; if it diverges wildly, investigate before trusting either.

## 3. Income approach — the Independent Investor Test (the CEILING)

This is the method most commercial reports add and the one that carries real legal weight, from
**Exacto Spring Corp. v. Commissioner, 196 F.3d 833 (7th Cir. 1999)** (Posner, J.) and applied
in **Menard, Inc. v. Commissioner (7th Cir. 2009)**. The doctrine roots trace to the classic
multi-factor test of **Mayson Mfg. Co. v. Commissioner, 178 F.2d 115 (6th Cir. 1949)**.

**The test:** would an *independent investor* be satisfied with their return on equity **after**
the owner is paid this compensation? If yes, the compensation is presumptively reasonable.

**Mechanics:**
1. Determine **shareholder equity / invested capital** (book value of equity, or capital the
   owner has at risk).
2. Determine the **return an independent investor would require** for a closely-held business of
   this risk — commonly **10–20%** (higher for small, risky, illiquid firms). State the basis.
3. Compute the company's **post-compensation return** = (net income after the proposed owner
   comp) ÷ equity.
4. **Interpret:**
   - Post-comp return **≥** required return → an investor is satisfied → comp is reasonable, and
     there is *room* for the salary. In the S-corp (comp-too-low) context this confirms the
     recommended salary doesn't starve investor return.
   - Comp so high that post-comp return **falls below** the required return → comp is likely
     **too high** (the ceiling). Pull it back toward the range the test supports.

The income approach mainly sets a **ceiling** and confirms affordability; it rarely drives the
number *up* for a service business, but it is powerful evidence that a figure is not excessive.

## Reconciliation — one figure from three

Do not average blindly. **Weight by relevance to this client** and explain the weighting:

| Client type | Cost | Market | Income |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hands-on owner-operator / service S-corp (typical) | High (≈60–70%) | Moderate (≈20–30%) | Check/ceiling (≈10%) |
| Capital-intensive or product company | Moderate | Moderate–High | Higher |
| High-profit relative to owner labor | Moderate | Moderate | Higher (ceiling binds) |

Steps:
1. State each approach's indicated value and range.
2. Note convergence or divergence and why.
3. Apply weights (with rationale) → **reconciled reasonable compensation**.
4. Confirm the reconciled figure passes the Independent Investor Test (post-comp return still
   acceptable) and sits within the cost-approach range.
5. Keep the firm's **defensible-low posture**: within the reconciled range, land low where the
   data corroborates it.

Document the whole reconciliation in the report's "Reconciliation" section so the single number
is visibly the product of three methods, not one.
